The fixed Diablo camera

Trade your eyeballed angle for the locked, looking-down view the whole game is seen through.

Why this, now

In 0001 you tilted the camera by feel just to see something. But the camera angle is the genre. Diablo, and every rogue-lite in its lineage, is read through one specific shot: high up, angled down, and never rotating. Lock that in and the game instantly looks like the thing we're making. This is the lens for everything that follows.

What makes a camera "Diablo-style"

Three properties, and only the first is new:

  1. A steep down-angle — roughly 45–55° below the horizon. High enough to see the battlefield around you, shallow enough to still see the fronts of things (so Gizmo and enemies read as characters, not floor tiles).
  2. Aimed at the action — pointed at the play area (for now, the origin where the box sits).
  3. Fixed — it does not spin when the character turns. The world rotates under a still camera; the camera never orbits the hero. This is the defining trait, and it's why a 3D model earns its keep here: the engine redraws Gizmo correctly from this one angle no matter which way he faces.

Angle and position are the same decision

Here's the one piece of math worth internalizing. If you want the camera to sit at distance d and look at the origin from an angle θ below horizontal, its position and its tilt are locked together:

position = (0,  d·sin θ,  d·cos θ)
rotation about X = −θ

Move it higher, it must tilt down more to keep aiming at the same spot. We'll use θ = 50° at d ≈ 15.6, which rounds to a clean position (0, 12, 10) with rotation X = −50°. (Sanity check: a ray from (0,12,10) tilted 50° down lands right back at the origin.)

The decision: perspective or orthographic?

A Camera3D has a Projection setting, and it changes the feel:

Perspective (the default): distant things shrink, like a real lens. Tuned by FOV (field of view, in degrees). It gives depth and a little drama — the modern Diablo look. A narrower FOV (~50, vs the default 75) flattens the distortion toward that clean ARPG feel without going fully flat.

Orthographic: no shrinking with distance — a box near you and far from you are drawn the same size. Tuned by Size (world units visible top-to-bottom). It reads as crisp and tactical (classic isometric strategy), but loses depth cues.

Recommendation: start with perspective, FOV ≈ 50 — it suits a character-driven gouache world. Then flip to Orthographic for ten seconds just to feel the difference. The choice is yours and easy to change later.

Build the slice — hands-on
  1. Open scenes/main.tscn and select your Camera3D.
  2. In the Inspector, set Transform → Position to (0, 12, 10).
  3. Set Transform → Rotation X to -50 (degrees). The camera now looks down at the box.
  4. Find Projection. Leave it on Perspective and set Fov to about 50.
  5. Press F5. You should be looking down on the box and floor at the signature angle — the floor reads as ground you stand on, not a wall.
  6. Experiment: switch Projection to Orthographic, set Size to about 16, Play again. Notice the floor's edges go parallel and the scene flattens. Then set it back to whichever you prefer.
  7. Ctrl+S.

Prefer I set it through the runtime so you can compare against your numbers? Say the word.

The thing to actually remember

Your Camera3D is a child of Main — the world — not a child of the box. That's deliberate. When Gizmo arrives (0003) and starts moving (0004), the camera keeps its angle no matter what he does. In 0005 we'll make it slide to follow his position — but it will keep this exact tilt forever. Fixed angle, movable position. That sentence is the whole camera design.

How you'll know it worked

Playing should feel like looking down into a diorama: you see the top and the front face of the box, the floor recedes gently (perspective) or stays perfectly even (orthographic), and the box sits clearly on the ground. If you're looking at the box edge-on or from the side, your rotation is off — it should be a negative X rotation near −50.

Done means…

Done = the locked down-angle view plays, and you can say in a sentence why the camera is parented to the world and not the character. Then I write the 0002 learning record and we bring Gizmo into the scene (0003).
