Gizmo · Lesson 0003 · The character

Gizmo enters the world

Swap the stand-in box for the real clanker — your own model, standing under your camera.

Why this, now

You have a stage (the floor) and a lens (the Diablo camera). Time for the star. The box was only ever a placeholder for where the character goes. Now we drop in gizmo.glb — the rigged model — and the game suddenly has a face. This is also your first time pulling an external 3D asset into a scene, which is how every prop, enemy, and pickup will arrive later.

An imported model is a scene, not just a mesh

When Godot imported gizmo.glb, it didn't make a single mesh — it built a whole little scene: a root node with the mesh, the materials, and the 53-bone skeleton (the rig) inside it. Godot treats that bundle as a reusable asset called a PackedScene. You don't edit it in place; you instance it — drop a copy into your world. The same asset could be instanced a hundred times (handy when we need a hundred enemies).

Two numbers that make this painless

I measured the model so you don't have to guess: Gizmo is about 2 metres tall, and his origin sits at his feet. That second part is the gift — placing him at (0, 0, 0) stands him exactly on the floor (which is also at y=0). No scaling, no sinking through the ground, no floating. (Contrast the box, which we had to lift to y=0.5 because its origin is at its center.)

Build the slice — hands-on
  1. Open scenes/main.tscn.
  2. In the FileSystem dock (bottom-left), open assets/ and find gizmo.glb.
  3. Drag gizmo.glb onto the Main node in the Scene dock. Godot adds it as a child — an instance of the model. (Equivalent: select Main → the chain-link Instantiate Child Scene button → pick gizmo.glb.)
  4. Rename the new node Gizmo.
  5. Set its Transform → Position to (0, 0, 0). He should stand on the floor, roughly where the box is.
  6. Select the old Box node and delete it (Del) — Gizmo has the stage now.
  7. Press F5. The clanker is standing in your game, seen down the Diablo camera. 🤖
  8. Ctrl+S.

Want me to instance and place him through the runtime instead, then walk the node tree it creates? Just ask.

If he looks dim or facing away

He's lit only by the Sun, which currently points straight down — so his top is lit and his face is shadowed. That's fine for now; we'll light the scene properly later. If you want a quick improvement, select Sun and give it a Rotation like X −45, Y −30 so light rakes across him. Facing direction we'll control with code in 0005 — don't worry about which way he looks yet.

How you'll know it worked

You should see a recognizable Gizmo standing on the floor, his feet on the ground (not sunk, not hovering), framed by the down-angled camera. If he's a tiny speck he got scaled down on drop; if he's sunk to the waist his position Y isn't 0. Both are one inspector field away.

Done means…

Done = Gizmo stands in the scene on his feet, and you can explain why dropping him at y=0 worked when the box needed y=0.5. Then I log 0003 and we give him a body and make him move (0004) — the first time the game feels like a game.
